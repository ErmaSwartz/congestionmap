Verdict

Amplified, not originated.

Across five datasets and four months of post-treatment data, NYC's congestion charge did real, measurable work — but the bigger redistribution it gets credit for was already underway. Here's what holds up, what doesn't, and what the next equity conversation should be about.

What the cordon actually did

Five findings that survive scrutiny

1 · Vehicle entries fell at the MTA crossings.

About 3.25 % fewer vehicles came across the nine MTA-tolled bridges and tunnels in the first four months of the cordon. Seven of nine crossings declined; the steepest drops were at the Verrazzano-Narrows and Marine Parkway. This is the cleanest single piece of evidence that the toll did real work on the inflow side. Caveat: it doesn't separate cars from trucks, and it doesn't include the four city-owned East River bridges (whose users pay the cordon toll by camera, but aren't counted in this MTA dataset).

2 · Soot inside the zone fell about 22 % more than at faraway monitors.

That's the comparison that matters most for people's health. PM2.5 — the fine particulate from combustion that actually gets into lungs — dropped sharply inside the cordon, even after subtracting whatever was happening to soot levels everywhere else. The 95 % confidence interval doesn't cross zero, so this isn't a noise reading. Caveat: NYCCAS recalibrated its measuring instrument in mid-2024, partway through the comparison period. Some of the apparent drop is the cordon, and some is the instrument changing.

3 · Smog over Manhattan dropped about 20 %, by satellite.

The Sentinel-5P satellite measures NO₂ in a column above each ~5-kilometer pixel. Over the cordon, after subtracting trend, the satellite saw a substantial drop. The confidence interval is wide because the cordon is small in satellite pixels and four months of post-treatment data isn't a lot. The number is consistent with a real effect; it can't fully rule out chance.

4 · The mode mix tilted slightly toward transit.

About 1.3 percentage points of all daily trips into the CBD shifted off the bridges-and-tunnels share and onto the bus share. Subway barely moved its share. Commuter rail and paratransit moved by sub-percentage-point amounts. Real, measurable, but far smaller than the headline framing of "people switched from cars to transit."

5 · Bus ridership grew about 13 % more than its trend predicted.

The only mode that clearly grew faster than the underlying recovery trend would have predicted. Subway, LIRR, Metro-North, and Staten Island Railway all came in below their projected lines — they grew, but slower than expected. The bus jump is consistent with outer-borough drivers — the people without good subway access — switching to the bus when driving in got expensive. Caveat: bus ridership is volatile enough that the projected line doesn't fit it well. Suggestive rather than precise.

What I had to walk back

Three findings that didn't hold up

A boundary "dead zone."

An earlier version of the taxi analysis used different time windows — full 2024 vs all post-2025 data — and showed taxi pickups falling in the quarter-mile band right outside the cordon. On the corrected, properly-matched four-month windows, that band actually grew slightly (+4 %). The boundary is the least-affected band, but not a declining one. The "dead zone" headline disappears.

A clean way to attribute the toll using Access-A-Ride as a control.

The natural way to prove the toll caused changes is to compare modes the toll affects against a mode it shouldn't — and show the first group changed more. Access-A-Ride looked perfect: paratransit, eligibility-based, used mostly for medical trips. People don't start riding it because of a downtown toll. But AAR grew 24.5 % on its own — faster than any other mode, for unrelated reasons (more people are eligible, the population is aging). Comparing other modes against AAR would make the toll look like it hurt every mode, which contradicts everything else. The comparison broke. We had to fall back to projected-trend estimates.

A clear weekday-versus-weekend signature.

The toll bites hardest on weekdays — that's when it's full price and traffic is heaviest. So weekday transit ridership should have grown more than weekend ridership, and weekday driving should have fallen more than weekend driving. Neither happened. Subway, LIRR, and Metro-North all grew slightly more on weekends. Bridge entries were essentially flat both days. The cordon's fingerprints in time-of-day data just aren't there. That's a null finding — but combined with the off-peak taxi result, it argues that whatever's shaping these patterns isn't concentrated in the hours the toll is on.

The bigger picture

Most of what looks like the cordon was already in motion

Most of what changed between early 2024 and early 2025 wasn't the cordon. Total trips into Manhattan went up 8.94 %, not down. Subway, bus, commuter rail were all on a steep recovery curve that started years before the toll. Yellow taxis had been gradually pushing further into outer boroughs as Uber and Lyft ate into their core market. The cordon's actual contribution is real, measurable, and a specific small slice of that bigger picture.

The clearest single piece of evidence is the off-peak taxi result. When the toll is discounted 75 % and the cordon is essentially off, the spatial spillover pattern is steeper, not weaker. Most of where the taxi pickups landed is being shaped by something other than the toll — and the toll is sitting on top of that, contributing a real but small tilt of its own.

For the next conversation

Where the equity discussion needs to happen

The 3 – 5 mile ring around the cordon — Long Island City, Williamsburg, Park Slope, the South Bronx — absorbed the strongest taxi-pickup growth. Whether that growth is policy-caused or recovery-caused, those are the neighborhoods absorbing the spillover, and they're the ones where the conversation about who bears the costs and benefits of this policy should land.

The air-quality result also matters here. PM2.5 inside the cordon fell sharply, and PM2.5 is among the most consequential pollutants for asthma, cardiovascular disease, and premature mortality. Whatever the policy did or didn't do to traffic, it appears to have done something real to the air over Manhattan. That's worth a lot, even if the magnitudes are softer than the satellite headline.

The honest summary, in three sentences

The cordon did real, measurable work on inflow vehicles, on PM2.5 inside Manhattan, and on the bus mode share. It did not, in its first four months, eliminate or even reduce total movement into the CBD. Most of what looks like a redistribution toward the outer boroughs was a redistribution that was already in motion before January 5, 2025.
