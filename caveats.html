A page of honest disclosures

What this analysis can, and can't, tell us.

Every number on this site has a scope. Some are clean, some are noisy, some shouldn't be read as causal at all. This page lays out what each dataset measures, what it doesn't measure, and which claims are weaker than the headlines might suggest.

The satellite's resolution

The satellite sees broad regional patterns, not blocks.

TROPOMI on Sentinel-5P measures the tropospheric column of NO₂ above each ~5×3 km pixel. The congestion zone polygon resolves to about 24 pixels; the rest of NYC has roughly 2,700. That's fine for the zone-vs-rest-of-NYC comparison and for the borough-scale patterns shown in the maps, but it's coarse for any neighborhood-level claim. What it changes: the air finding has to be read at the regional scale it was measured at. Block-level interpretations on the maps are unsafe even when the choropleths look detailed.

The 16-month windows

The analysis uses season-matched windows, not the v1 four-month framing.

The pre window runs January 2023 – April 2024 (16 months). The post window runs January 2025 – April 2026 (16 months). Each contains the Jan–Apr sequence twice, so winter peaks and summer troughs cancel out cleanly. The earlier v1 analysis used a tighter four-month real-vs-placebo window; this version is longer, season-balanced, and produces smaller but more defensible numbers. What it changes: several v1 headline numbers don't survive the longer windows. The +160% taxi growth collapses to ~+6 pp; the −22% air number resolves to a smaller, cleaner −3.9 pp zone-attributable signal. The longer windows are the right call but the headlines moved.

One pollutant

NO₂ only — not PM2.5, not ozone, not anything else.

The satellite measures NO₂ — the combustion gas that comes mostly from tailpipes. It does not measure PM2.5 (fine particulate matter), ground-level ozone, or any of the other pollutants that contribute to NYC's air-quality story. NO₂ is a useful proxy for traffic-emission patterns, but it isn't the whole air-quality picture. What it changes: a finding that NO₂ dropped over the congestion zone is not the same as a finding that "the air got cleaner" along every dimension. PM2.5 in particular may be moving in the opposite direction in some parts of the city — see the South Bronx caveat below.

The South Bronx PM2.5 finding

Ground monitors saw PM2.5 rise where my satellite saw nothing change.

While I was finishing this analysis, Columbia and South Bronx Unite (May 2026) released findings showing PM2.5 rose at 17 of 19 ground monitors in the South Bronx during the congestion zone's first year, with the sharpest increases near major expressways. Truck rerouting around the toll zone is a plausible mechanism for that pattern. What it changes: both findings can be true — different pollutants, different instruments, different scales. My satellite analysis can't see neighborhood-block changes that ground sensors can. The fairer summary is that the congestion zone's effects were real, distributionally uneven, and the populations that already bear the heaviest pollution burden may have absorbed costs this analysis can't measure.

The placebo assumption

The DiD assumes the 2024-vs-2023 comparison is a reasonable counterfactual.

The placebo design depends on the assumption that the year-on-year drift between 2023 and 2024 is a reasonable estimate of what would have happened between 2024 and 2025 without the congestion zone. If the underlying trend changed shape between those two transitions for non-policy reasons — a national fleet-turnover shift, a regional weather anomaly, a methodology change in the source data — the DiD partly attributes that change to the policy. What it changes: the placebo controls for slow-moving background drift but not for one-off events that hit only the post-period. None has been documented for NYC NO₂ in 2025, but the assumption is structural to the analysis and worth naming.

Bus service expansion confound

The bus DiD is partly a service-expansion story, not pure mode shift.

The bus DiD of +10.0 pp is the largest transit signal in the project, but in June 2025 — midway through the post-period — the MTA rolled out a major service expansion in Queens (a borough-wide redesign) plus 16 enhanced routes citywide funded by congestion-toll revenue. Some unknown share of the +10 pp is people responding to more service, not just to a higher cost of driving. What it changes: the bus number captures the policy design working as intended (toll plus better alternatives) but it's not a clean estimate of pure behavioral mode shift. Subway's cleaner +3.9 pp is the better number for the latter.

No FHV / Uber / Lyft

Yellow taxis only — rideshare substitution can't be ruled out.

The taxi-spillover analysis (Chapter 03) uses NYC TLC's yellow-taxi trip records. It does not include for-hire-vehicle records — Uber, Lyft, Via — which are now substantially larger than yellow taxis by trip volume in NYC. FHV trip records at zone resolution were not available within the project's timeframe. What it changes: the modal-substitution-at-the-edge hypothesis fails on yellow taxis, but the broader hypothesis about rideshare is unresolved. Anyone with TLC's full FHV trip files could re-test on Uber and Lyft, and a different result there would be a different paper.

Taxi zones as analytical units

NYC taxi zones are imperfect demographic units.

The neighborhood-level analyses in this project use the 263 NYC TLC taxi zones — a geometry drawn for taxi dispatch, not population analysis. A zone may bundle dissimilar neighborhoods together; some zones contain more residents than others by an order of magnitude. What it changes: at the satellite's coarse resolution, a finer geometry would give the appearance of precision the data can't actually deliver. Taxi zones are adequate for this scale and consistent with how prior NYC transportation work has aggregated. They wouldn't be adequate for ground-monitor or block-level analysis.

Bridges, post-April 2025

The MTA stopped publishing per-plaza counts during the post-period.

The MTA stopped publishing per-plaza daily bridge counts after April 2025. From May 2025 forward, the bridges analysis uses the aggregate daily total summed across all nine MTA crossings. The headline DiD is robust to that change, but per-crossing breakdowns from May 2025 onward are not available. What it changes: the bridges story stays at the citywide-aggregate level, which is enough to detect the −1.75 pp signal but rules out questions about which individual crossings absorbed the change.

What this project would need next

For a sharper answer.

For-hire-vehicle records at zone resolution, to test substitution on the larger fleet. Surface air monitors inside Manhattan close to the congestion zone, paired with the satellite. PM2.5 and ozone alongside NO₂. Per-plaza MTA bridge counts continued past April 2025. Route-level transit ridership to separate service-expansion effects from mode-shift effects. A second similar city without a toll, as a cross-city control. And, most of all, a longer post-treatment window — three more years would tighten the borderline confidence intervals in this project considerably, and would let the equity question be asked with the time depth it deserves.

Honest about the data is honest about the conclusion. The congestion zone did real work, of a smaller and more specific kind than headlines on either side suggest. All three of those statements are simultaneously true.
