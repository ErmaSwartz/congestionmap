A page of honest disclosures

What this analysis can, and can't, tell us.

Every number on this site has a scope. Some are clean, some are noisy, some shouldn't be read as causal at all. This page lays out what each dataset measures, what it doesn't measure, and which claims are weaker than the headlines might suggest.

The biggest gap

No Uber, no Lyft, no FHV at zone resolution.

The taxi spillover analysis (Chapter 04) uses NYC TLC's yellow-taxi trip records. It does not include for-hire-vehicle records — Uber, Lyft, Via — which are now substantially larger than yellow taxis by trip volume in NYC. FHV trip records at zone resolution were not available within the timeframe of this analysis.

This matters for the modal substitution null. The hypothesis predicted yellow-taxi pickups would concentrate at the cordon edge. They didn't, and the geometry of the data is good enough to rule that pattern out at the resolution the yellow fleet captures. But it remains possible that displaced commuters substituted onto rideshare in a pattern this analysis cannot see. The honest read of Chapter 04 is "the spatial-gradient version of the substitution hypothesis fails, and the broader hypothesis is unresolved."

Bridges & tunnels

Mixed-vehicle counts, only at MTA crossings, with a mid-window data change.

The MTA count includes every vehicle that crosses one of its nine tolled plazas — cars, taxis, vans, trucks, intercity buses. The headline DiD of −1.75 pp means "1.75 percentage points fewer vehicles of any kind, beyond placebo drift," not specifically "1.75 pp fewer cars."

The four East River bridges (Brooklyn, Manhattan, Williamsburg, Queensboro) are city-owned and not in the MTA dataset. Drivers crossing them to enter the cordon zone still pay the toll — it's collected by camera — but they don't show up in the MTA's plaza counts. So this analysis can see the price effect at the MTA crossings but can't fully resolve substitution onto free crossings.

The MTA stopped publishing per-plaza daily counts after April 2025. From May 2025 forward, this analysis uses the aggregate daily total summed across all nine crossings. The headline DiD is robust to that change; per-crossing breakdowns from May 2025 onward are not available.

Sensitivity check: a tighter 12-month real-vs-placebo window (Jan–Dec 2025 vs Jan–Dec 2024, instead of the full 15-month real comparison) shrinks the bridges DiD to +0.18 pp [−1.4, +1.8] — straddling zero. The bridges finding is at the edge of detectability and should be read as a small effect, defensible but not a cornerstone.

Transit ridership

The bus DiD includes a service expansion confound.

The bus DiD of +10.0 pp is the largest and cleanest cordon-attributable signal in the project. The CI excludes zero with room to spare. But: in June 2025, midway through the post-period, the MTA rolled out a major service expansion in Queens (a borough-wide redesign) plus 16 enhanced routes funded by congestion-toll revenue. Some fraction of the +10 pp is people responding to more service, not just to a higher cost of driving.

Mode-shift-induced and service-induced ridership cannot be cleanly separated with the data MTA publishes. The honest framing: the cordon both raised the cost of driving and improved the alternative; the DiD captures both, and the combination is partly the cordon's policy design working as intended.

MTA Daily Ridership is system-wide totals by mode. There is no station-level or route-level detail, so I cannot check whether the bus increase concentrates on cordon-adjacent routes — which would tighten the modal-shift attribution.

The negative DiDs on LIRR, Metro-North, and Staten Island Railway (between −6 and −7 pp) are best read as recovery saturation rather than cordon effects. The placebo year shows those modes were on a steeper recovery curve in 2024 than they were able to sustain in 2025. They were running out of riders to recover.

Satellite NO₂ (TROPOMI)

It's a column, and the cordon is a few pixels wide.

Sentinel-5P TROPOMI measures the tropospheric column of NO₂ above each ~5 × 3 km pixel. The cordon polygon contains 24 pixels; the rest of NYC has 817. So I can compare cordon vs. rest-of-NYC and cordon vs. borough, but I cannot finely resolve neighborhoods inside the cordon — anything inside is averaged into one signal.

NO₂ column is correlated with street-level NO₂, but they are not the same thing. The satellite tells me how much NO₂ sits in the air above the cordon, not what people are breathing at the curb. A health story about pedestrians and cyclists would need surface monitors close to the cordon, which the federal EPA network does not have anywhere in Manhattan.

NO₂ is also partly weather-driven. Wind direction, mixing height, and storms all swing the column day-to-day. The 15-month windows on each side smooth most of that out, but the placebo-year null (cordon-vs-rest gap ≈ 0 with no policy in effect) is the load-bearing reassurance that the real-comparison gap is signal, not weather.

Methodology

The placebo design vs. the v1 same-months design.

The earlier v1 analysis used a matched-calendar design: Jan 5 – Apr 30 in 2024 vs the same window in 2025. That is a clean way to control for seasonality, but it cannot separate the cordon's contribution from any non-policy drift — for instance, post-pandemic ridership recovery, fleet electrification, or yellow-taxi market-share clawback against FHV.

The v2 design replaces that with a placebo year. The real comparison runs October 2023 – December 2024 (458 days, pre-policy) against January 2025 – March 2026 (451 days, post-policy). The placebo runs 2023 against 2024 — the same calendar comparison structure with no policy in either year. The DiD subtracts the placebo from the real change. Whatever survives is the cordon-attributable signal.

Two consequences. First: several v1 numbers shrink dramatically when the placebo subtracts out background trends — most notably the +160% taxi figure, which collapses to roughly +6 pp (and uniform across distance, defeating the substitution hypothesis). Second: the design depends on the assumption that 2024 vs 2023 is a reasonable counterfactual for what 2025 vs 2024 would have looked like without the cordon. If the underlying trend changed shape between those two transitions for non-policy reasons, the DiD partially attributes that change to the cordon. The bridge sensitivity check — tightening the window to 12 months — is the clearest place that assumption shows.

What this project would need next

For a sharper answer.

  • For-hire-vehicle records (Uber, Lyft, Via) at zone resolution, to test substitution on the larger fleet
  • Per-plaza MTA bridge counts continued past April 2025
  • East-River bridge counts (Brooklyn, Manhattan, Williamsburg, Queensboro) so the substitution-onto-free-crossings hypothesis is testable directly
  • Route-level or station-level MTA ridership, to separate cordon-funded bus service expansion from cordon-induced mode shift
  • A surface-level air monitor inside Manhattan, near or in the cordon
  • A second-best comparison city — somewhere similar in size and density that didn't impose a toll — to act as a cross-city control for non-policy trends
  • A longer post-treatment window (three more years would tighten the borderline CIs in this project considerably)

Honest about the data is honest about the conclusion. The cordon did real work, of a smaller and more specific kind than headlines on either side suggest. All three of those statements are simultaneously true.
