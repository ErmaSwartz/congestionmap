Chapter 04 · Verdict

What the evidence suggests, what remains uncertain, and where the analysis reaches its limits.

The clearest finding

The evidence suggests congestion pricing was associated with measurable improvements in air quality over Manhattan's congestion zone. Satellite-measured nitrogen dioxide declined more over Manhattan below 60th Street than over the rest of the city, and the placebo comparison suggests this gap exceeds ordinary year-to-year atmospheric variation.

After adjusting for background drift, the estimated zone-attributable improvement is approximately 3.9 percentage points. The signal is modest in magnitude but spatially coherent and methodologically robust. It is the strongest finding in this project.

Findings that proved smaller or conditional

The bridges difference-in-differences estimate was approximately −1.75 percentage points, with a confidence interval that narrowly excludes zero. Vehicle entries declined, but the magnitude is on the same order as ordinary year-to-year fluctuation.

The transit findings split. Buses gained an estimated +10 percentage points above the placebo trajectory, but that result overlaps with the June 2025 MTA service expansion in Queens and the addition of 16 enhanced routes citywide. Some share of the increase reflects improved service rather than pure response to the toll. Subway gained a cleaner +3.9 percentage points. Commuter-rail modes (LIRR, Metro-North, Staten Island Railway) declined in both the real and placebo windows, suggesting recovery saturation rather than a congestion-zone effect.

The taxi-spillover hypothesis was tested across four predictions. None of the expected signatures — proximity to subway hubs near the cordon, peak commute hours, weekday-only patterns, hub-to-zone trip pairs — appeared consistently in yellow-taxi data. Yellow-taxi pickups increased roughly uniformly across distance bands from the zone. The most-feared spillover pattern did not appear in the data this analysis can see.

What the analysis did not measure

The Columbia and South Bronx Unite study, released as this analysis was being completed, reports PM2.5 increases at 17 of 19 ground monitors in the South Bronx during the congestion zone's first year, especially near major expressways. Truck rerouting is a plausible mechanism. The satellite used here cannot resolve those neighborhood-block changes.

FHV and Uber/Lyft trip records were unavailable at zone resolution. Indoor air, individual exposure, and longer-term trajectories are outside this dataset's scope. None of those gaps are evidence that the corresponding effects did not occur. They are evidence that this analysis cannot speak to them.

A measured interpretation

Congestion pricing appears to have produced a measurable, spatially coherent improvement in NYC's nitrogen dioxide pollution over the area where it operates. The improvement was modest in magnitude and unevenly distributed across neighborhoods — most pronounced relative to higher-income, less-transit-oriented neighborhoods, and smallest relative to lower-income, transit-dependent neighborhoods that experienced similar declines on their own.

The findings do not support a story about congestion pricing transforming New York's air. They support a more specific story: a policy producing a detectable signal in one pollutant, over one zone, with effects that varied by neighborhood and that this analysis cannot fully decompose.

The next stages — health, equity, and time — sit beyond what this dataset can measure.

What further analysis would require

Stronger conclusions would benefit from ground-monitor data alongside satellite measurements, FHV records at zone resolution, expanded coverage to PM2.5 and ozone, and a longer post-treatment window. The placebo comparison would remain the load-bearing methodological choice; everything else this project did becomes more defensible because of it.
