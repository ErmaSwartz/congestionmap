A spatial study

NYC Congestion Pricing:
Where Does the Traffic Go?

On January 5, 2025, New York City switched on the first cordon-style congestion charge in the United States — covering Manhattan south of 60th Street.

Traffic doesn't disappear when its price goes up. It moves. The question this project asks is where, and how unevenly.

02 · The raw outcome

But pickups grew almost everywhere

Aggregating 89.6 million yellow-taxi trips to 243 taxi zones (237 in the regime-split GWR, after a per-regime volume filter), the picture flips. Median pickup change is +160%.

Only five zones (in pink) actually declined. Everywhere else, pickups rose — but unevenly. This is the substrate the model has to explain.

03 · A first cut

Concentric rings around the cordon

Before reaching for a model, slice the city into distance bands measured outward from the cordon edge.

Each zone gets the distance from its centroid to the nearest point on the Congestion Relief Zone, in EPSG:2263 — the projection NYC defaults to.

04 · The ring story

A boundary "dead zone" — and a 3–5 mile peak

The narrow strip just outside the cordon is the only band of any width that declined — drivers staying back from the boundary.

The strongest growth lands 3–5 miles out: Long Island City, Williamsburg, Park Slope, the South Bronx — transit-accessible neighborhoods just beyond the toll.

05 · Why a global model fails

One slope can't tell this story

OLS R²
0.49
GWR R²
0.76

A global OLS reports a clean −5.3 %/mile distance-decay during tolled hours (p < 0.001), and a much weaker −3.3 %/mile when the toll is essentially off (p = 0.16). It explains about half the variance in pickup change either way.

The scatter is too wide for any single line. The other half is spatial — distance behaves differently depending on where you stand.

06 · The headline finding

The distance effect flips sign across the city

Local distance coefficient, range across 237 zones (peak regime)
−39 to +176

Geographically Weighted Regression (mgwr, adaptive bisquare kernel, bw = 50) fits a separate local slope at every zone.

Inner Manhattan and the Hudson waterfront read near-zero or negative — pickups are up almost everywhere there, regardless of distance. Outer Brooklyn, Queens, the Bronx, and Staten Island read strongly positive: the further from the cordon, the more pickups grew.

119 of 237 zones (50%) have a coefficient that is statistically distinguishable from zero.

07 · Time of day · peak

Peak hours look like the all-hours field

Re-running GWR on peak-hour pickups produces a coefficient field that looks remarkably similar to the all-hours version. Inner Manhattan and the Hudson waterfront still read near zero or slightly negative; outer boroughs still run strongly positive. GWR R² is 0.76 in this regime.

The expected sharpening when the toll is most expensive does not materialize: the cordon's price signal is producing the spatial pattern, but only a small slice of it.

08 · Time of day · off-peak

Off-peak: the surprise

Re-running on off-peak pickups, when the toll is discounted by 75%, produces a coefficient field with a similar spatial pattern but a steeper average gradient. Mean local β is +19 — twice the peak value. GWR R² is 0.77, slightly higher than peak.

This is the surprise: when the cordon is essentially off, distance from it predicts pickup change more steeply than when it is on. The pattern is being driven by something other than the toll alone — likely a combination of post-pandemic taxi recovery weighted toward late-night airport service, and outer-borough demand growth that was already underway.

09 · Identification

Differencing the two regimes isolates the toll

To isolate the toll's own contribution from broader 2024-to-2025 trends, the two regimes are differenced at every zone: β(peak) − β(off-peak). Anything that affected both regimes equally — secular taxi recovery, weather — drops out. What remains is the cordon-attributable component of the local distance gradient.

The differenced field is much weaker than either single-regime field. The mean is −9.9 percentage points per mile, and the field is mostly negative across the city. At the global level, the toll adds approximately 2 percentage points per mile of additional distance-decay during tolled hours (peak OLS minus off-peak OLS) — a real and statistically significant effect, but a modest part of the broader spatial pattern.

10 · Model fit

GWR beats OLS in both regimes — by the same margin

Comparing the two regimes side by side, GWR raises R² from about 0.5 to 0.76 in the peak regime and from 0.45 to 0.77 in off-peak. Letting the slope vary in space recovers a substantial amount of explanatory power that no single OLS slope can capture, and the magnitude of that improvement is essentially identical across regimes.

The spatial-heterogeneity story works equally well whether the toll is on or off — which is the central reason to interpret the cordon's contribution as additive rather than dominant.

11 · Where the model fails

Local R² names the next investigation

Mean local R² · max local R²
0.51 · 0.87

The fit peaks on the southern and outer-borough fringe — Rockaways, southwestern Brooklyn, Staten Island — where distance and baseline volume genuinely explain most of what is happening.

It collapses below 0.20 in upper Manhattan and the central Bronx. Those zones are driven by something other than distance to the cordon: income, car ownership, road class, free-crossing proximity, PLUTO land use. They are the targets for round two.

12 · Takeaway

The cordon amplifies, not originates

Bridge entries down 3.25%. Pickups up almost everywhere, with the strongest growth 3 to 5 miles from the cordon. A global slope of −5.3 %/mile during tolled hours that hides a local range from −39 to +176.

Congestion pricing does produce a measurable additional distance-decay, but the broader spillover pattern was already in motion: yellow-taxi demand was redistributing toward outer boroughs whether or not the cordon went on. The policy amplifies a dynamic rather than originating it. The fringe and outer-borough zones that absorbed the most demand are where a redistributive-equity analysis of the policy should look next.
