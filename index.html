▸ Start here

On January 5, 2025, New York City became the first U.S. city to charge drivers a toll just for entering part of downtown. Drive into Manhattan below 60th Street, and you paid $9 during the day and $2.25 at night. The idea was to push fewer cars onto the streets, more people onto the subway, and cleaner air into everyone's lungs.

The promise was big and the political stakes are bigger. More than a year on, I pulled five datasets to ask what actually changed in the first four months of the policy, the calendar window where we have a clean comparison against 2024. The answer matters for the people who pay the toll, the people who breathe the air, and the policy folks deciding whether to extend the model to other corridors. The short answer is more interesting than yes or no. The cordon did real work. But a lot of what people are giving it credit for was already in motion before it switched on.

Did fewer cars come in?
About 3 % fewer vehicles at every MTA bridge and tunnel, but only the MTA-tolled crossings count, not the city-owned East River bridges.

02 · The Taxis
Where did the rides go?
The median taxi zone roughly doubled its pickups. The biggest growth was a few miles outside the cordon, not right at the boundary.
+9%
03 · The Modes
Did people switch to transit?
Total trips into Manhattan went up, not down. The mode mix tilted ~1.3 pp from bridges onto buses.
−22%
04 · The Air
Did the air get cleaner?
Soot inside the cordon fell sharply. A satellite says smog over Manhattan dropped too. Two instruments, same direction.

05 · The Twist
Was it really the toll?
Mostly, no. The same patterns show up at night when the toll is mostly off. Most of the geography isn't the cordon's doing.

▸ Things this project can't see

Yellow taxi data doesn't include Uber and Lyft. The MTA's bridge counts don't include the city-owned East River bridges (Brooklyn, Manhattan, Williamsburg, Queensboro), so we can see traffic shift away from MTA tunnels but not where it lands. The closest air monitor with a working pollution sensor is in the Bronx, not Manhattan. So when we talk about "fewer cars" or "cleaner air," we're really talking about some cars and some air.
