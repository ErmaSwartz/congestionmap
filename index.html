Fifteen months into NYC's congestion toll. What the bridges, transit, taxis, and air actually show — measured against a placebo year where no policy was in effect. The cordon worked through modal shift, not displacement. It did less than critics feared and less than its proponents promised.
WHERE ▸ Manhattan, south of 60th St
WHEN ▸ Started Jan 5, 2025
COMPARED TO ▸ A placebo year (2024 vs 2023, no policy in effect)
▸ Start here

On January 5, 2025, New York City became the first U.S. city to charge drivers a toll just for entering part of downtown. Drive into Manhattan below 60th Street, and you paid $9 during the day and $2.25 at night. The promise was fewer cars, more transit, cleaner air. The fear was that traffic would just reroute — onto the bridges that don't charge, into adjacent neighborhoods, into yellow cabs picking up displaced commuters at transit hubs.

More than a year on, I pulled four datasets and asked a sharper question than "did things change?" Most things change for many reasons — weather, fuel prices, post-pandemic recovery, seasonal patterns. To attribute a change to the cordon, you have to compare it against what would have happened anyway.

So I built a placebo: a 12-month comparison of 2024 vs 2023, before any policy was in effect. Whatever showed up in the placebo year is background drift. Whatever shows up above the placebo, in the real comparison, is what the cordon plausibly did. The short answer is that the cordon did real work, but a smaller and more specific kind of work than headline coverage suggests.

▸ Four findings that tell the story
▸ Cars crossing in
−1.75 pp
Cordon-attributable drop in MTA bridge & tunnel volume, after subtracting placebo drift. Modest but real.
▸ Bus ridership
+10.0 pp
Above its placebo trajectory. Subway also up (+3.9 pp). The cordon's modal-shift effect is bigger than its vehicle-reduction effect.
▸ NO₂ over the cordon
−9.6%
Satellite-measured. The rest of NYC dropped 6.5%; the cordon-specific gap is 3.1 pp. The placebo year was near-zero.
▸ Yellow-taxi spillover
Not detected
No spatial gradient with distance from cordon. The modal substitution hypothesis was tested across four predictions and rejected.

Three real findings, one walked-back, one null. The cordon worked, but through a narrower mechanism than its loudest advocates or critics described.
▸ Things this project can't see

Yellow-taxi data doesn't include Uber and Lyft, and FHV trip records weren't available at zone resolution within the timeframe. That's the largest single gap — rideshare substitution is a plausible response that this analysis can't detect. The MTA stopped publishing per-plaza bridge counts after April 2025, so the bridges analysis uses aggregate daily totals from May 2025 forward. The satellite (TROPOMI Sentinel-5P) has a native footprint of about 5×3 km, which limits how finely between-neighborhood differences can be resolved. The bus DiD partly reflects cordon-funded service expansion (Queens redesign + 16 route enhancements, June 2025) — it's not a clean behavioral mode-shift estimate. Full caveats →

▸ How this is different from v1

An earlier version of this site analyzed the first four months of the policy against the same months in 2024. That comparison showed real effects but couldn't separate the cordon's contribution from background drift. The current analysis runs a 15-month real comparison (Oct 2023 – Mar 2026) against a 12-month placebo (2024 vs 2023, no policy in effect). Several headline numbers moved meaningfully: the +160% taxi growth turns out to be largely background trend; the −22% pollution number resolves to a smaller, cleaner +3.1 pp cordon-specific NO₂ gap; the "everything got busier" framing gives way to a clearer modal-shift story.
