A look at NYC's congestion toll — four months in, across the bridges, the taxis, the subways, and the air.

▸ Start here

On January 5, 2025, New York City became the first U.S. city to charge drivers a toll just for entering part of downtown. If you drove a car into Manhattan below 60th Street, you paid $9 during the day and $2.25 at night. The idea was to push fewer cars onto the streets, more people onto the subway, and cleaner air into everyone's lungs.

Four months in, I wanted to know what actually changed. Did fewer cars come into Manhattan? Did the taxi traffic just shift somewhere else? Did people switch to the subway? Did the air get noticeably cleaner? Was any of it really because of the toll?

The short answer is more interesting than yes or no. The cordon did real work — but a lot of what people are giving it credit for was already in motion before it switched on. Here's how I got there.

▸ Six chapters · read in order, or jump
01 · The Bridges
Did fewer cars come in?
Yes — about 3 % fewer at every MTA bridge and tunnel. But that only counts some of the cars and none of the free crossings.
02 · The Taxis
Where did the rides actually go?
Yellow-cab pickups jumped almost everywhere. The biggest growth was a few miles outside Manhattan — not right at the toll.
03 · The Modes
Did people switch to transit?
Total trips into Manhattan went up, not down. A small slice — about a percent — moved from cars to buses.
04 · The Air
Did the air actually get cleaner?
Soot inside the toll zone dropped about 22 %. A satellite says smog over Manhattan dropped too. The numbers are wobbly, but consistent.
05 · The Twist
Was it really the toll?
Mostly — no. The same patterns show up at night when the toll is mostly off. Recovery from COVID is doing most of the work.
Verdict
What it actually did
The toll did real things — fewer cars, cleaner air, more buses. But the bigger story was already in motion before it switched on.
▸ Things this project can't see

Yellow taxi data doesn't include Uber and Lyft. Bridge counts don't include the free East River bridges (Brooklyn, Manhattan, Williamsburg, Queensboro). The closest air monitor with a working pollution sensor is in the Bronx, not Manhattan. So when we talk about "fewer cars" or "cleaner air," we're really talking about some cars and some air.
