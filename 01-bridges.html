Chapter 01 · The Bridges

Did fewer cars actually come into Manhattan?

If the toll worked the way its supporters hoped, you'd see it first at the bridges and tunnels — fewer drivers crossing in. The MTA counts every vehicle that comes across nine of those crossings, every day. Here's what those counts say.

The headline
Vehicles crossing into NYC at MTA bridges and tunnels · same months, 2024 vs 2025
−3.25%
A small but real drop. About three percent fewer crossings in the first four months after the toll switched on.

That's not a huge number — but it's the cleanest piece of evidence in this whole project that the toll did something. Seven of the nine MTA crossings dropped. The two that fell hardest both serve South Brooklyn and the Rockaways: the Verrazzano-Narrows (down nearly 9 %) and the Marine Parkway. The only crossing that grew was the Henry Hudson, way up the West Side, which is far enough from the toll zone that some drivers might have rerouted to use it.

What this number can't tell us

Three things to keep in mind

It counts every vehicle, not just cars.

The MTA's number includes cars, taxis, trucks, vans, intercity buses — anything paying a toll at those nine plazas. So "3 % fewer" means "3 % fewer vehicles of any kind," not specifically "3 % fewer cars." A truck swap or a bus schedule change is in there too.

It misses the East River bridges.

Four of the busiest crossings into Manhattan — the Brooklyn, Manhattan, Williamsburg, and Queensboro — are free. The city owns them, not the MTA, and they don't show up in this dataset. Which is a problem, because if a driver used to take the Queens Midtown Tunnel and now takes the free Queensboro Bridge instead to skip the cordon, the data here would show a tunnel decline. That looks like "fewer cars," but the cars haven't actually disappeared — they've just moved over.

It doesn't tell us what happened next.

If you're a driver who didn't make a trip you would have made before, where did you end up instead? On the subway? On a bus? At home? Stuck on a bridge in Queens? The bridge counts don't say. To answer that, we have to look at the other modes.

Fewer cars came in — but only the kinds the MTA tracks, and only at the crossings that charge a toll. To know what really happened, we have to look at where everyone else went.
