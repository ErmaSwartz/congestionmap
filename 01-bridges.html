Chapter 01 · The Bridges

Did fewer cars come in?

Cordon-attributable change · MTA bridges & tunnels · DiD
−1.75 pp
Real-comparison drop minus placebo drift. 95% bootstrap CI: [−3.33, −0.12].

Real comparison: −1.3% change in vehicles per day, across all nine MTA crossings. Placebo: +0.45% — basically flat. The placebo says traffic was on a slight upward drift on its own. The real comparison reverses that drift. Subtract one from the other and you get the cordon's signature on the bridges.

The cordon-attributable difference clears zero, just barely. A small effect, defensible but not bulletproof.

That qualifier matters. Tighten the window to a strictly 12-month real-vs-placebo (2025 vs 2024) and the DiD shrinks to +0.18 pp with a CI of [−1.4, +1.8] — straddling zero. Read this chapter as "small, real-ish, at the edge of detectability." Not as a cornerstone result, and not as nothing.

The bridges aren't where most of the effect lives.

The headline coverage of the cordon — fewer cars at the bridges — is the easiest claim to make and the smallest one to defend. A ~1% reduction in MTA crossings is hard to feel walking around midtown. The next chapter is where the policy did most of its work.

What I can't see

Three quick caveats. The MTA's count is mixed-vehicle (cars, taxis, vans, trucks, intercity buses) — "1.3% fewer vehicles" is not "1.3% fewer cars." The four city-owned East River bridges aren't in the dataset, so substitution onto free crossings is partly invisible. And per-plaza counts stopped after April 2025; from May 2025 forward this analysis uses the aggregate daily total. Full caveats →
