Chapter 01 · The Bridges

Did fewer cars actually come into Manhattan?

If the toll worked the way its supporters hoped, you'd see it first at the bridges and tunnels, fewer drivers crossing in. The MTA counts every vehicle at nine of those crossings, every day. Here's what those counts say.

Vehicles into NYC at MTA bridges & tunnels · Jan – Apr 2024 vs 2025
−3.25%
A small but real drop, and the cleanest single piece of evidence in this whole project that the toll did something.
The pattern

Seven of nine crossings declined

The two that fell hardest, the Verrazzano-Narrows (down nearly 9 %) and the Marine Parkway, both serve commutes from South Brooklyn and the Rockaways. The only crossing that grew was the Henry Hudson, way up on Manhattan's West Side, far enough from the toll zone that some drivers may have rerouted to use it instead.

The Henry Hudson rose 2.6 %, the lone outlier on the upside. It's the only MTA crossing far enough north of 60th Street to plausibly serve as a workaround for the cordon. That makes the rise interesting: it looks like a substitution effect, not noise.

What this number can't tell us

Three things to keep in mind

It counts every vehicle, not just cars.

The MTA's number includes cars, taxis, trucks, vans, intercity buses, anything paying a toll at those nine plazas. So "3 % fewer" means "3 % fewer vehicles of any kind," not specifically "3 % fewer cars." A truck swap or a bus schedule change is in there too.

It misses the East River bridges.

Four of the busiest crossings into Manhattan, the Brooklyn, Manhattan, Williamsburg, and Queensboro, are city-owned, not MTA-owned. They don't have toll plazas of their own and don't show up in this dataset. (To be clear: vehicles using them to enter the cordon zone do pay the congestion charge, there's just no separate bridge toll, and no MTA plaza counting them.) That's a problem, because if a driver used to take the Queens Midtown Tunnel and now takes the Queensboro Bridge to enter Manhattan north of 60th Street, the MTA's data would show a tunnel decline. That looks like "fewer cars," but the cars haven't actually disappeared, they've just moved over.

It doesn't tell us what happened next.

If you're a driver who didn't make a trip you would have made before, where did you end up instead? On the subway? On a bus? At home? Stuck on a bridge in Queens? The bridge counts don't say. To answer that, we have to look at the other modes.

Fewer vehicles came in, but only the kinds the MTA tracks, and only at the nine crossings it charges a toll on. To know what really happened, we have to look at where everyone else went.
